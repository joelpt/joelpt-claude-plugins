# End Goals

The anchors that calibrate every other decision in this plan.
These change rarely.
When they change, it is a deliberate event recorded in `REVIEWS/` and reflected in an ADR.

## EG-1: Increase the number of awakened beings on the planet

**Working definition:**
A being is "more awakened" to the extent that they directly perceive the nature of experience with reduced reactivity, increased equanimity, and increased compassionate responsiveness.
This is not a religious or sectarian claim — many traditions and many secular practices produce movement along this dimension.

**Theory of change (refined):**

1. **Access bottleneck:** Most people who would benefit from contemplative practice never encounter it because existing offerings (traditional centers, apps, coaching) require upfront capital, time commitment, or cultural fluency they lack. Barriers: cost, intimidation, poor contextual fit (beginner apps assume no prior practice; intermediate resources assume solitary retreat access).

2. **Addressable leverage:** AI-assisted personalized contemplative coaching can reduce friction on three vectors:
   - Cost: marginally-distributed AI can cost <$1–10/user/month vs. $100+/retreat or $20+/app subscription
   - Accessibility: asynchronous, private, adaptive to user schedule and language
   - Scaffolding: personalized feedback loop within a practice (error-correction) that traditional apps and teachers cannot provide at scale

3. **Quality gate:** Depth is maintained by (a) building curriculum from established traditions (not inventing new ones), (b) early pilots with experienced practitioners to detect "spiritual McDonald's" drift, (c) honest measurement (retreat-validated baseline for "awakeness" markers; control cohorts).

4. **Initial target:** Beginners without prior practice who have contemplative interest but low execution. Smaller cohort than mass-market self-help, but higher signal-to-noise.

**Confidence:** 0.65 — addressable bottleneck is real; AI-assisted delivery is plausible; but measurement of "awakeness" is hard and risk of inauthenticity is genuine.

**Open questions (linked to Q-001, Q-002):**
- Early target population specificity: beginners, intermediate stuck-plateau practitioners, or both?
- Evaluation: what criteria distinguish real benefit from felt-benefit or placebo response?

## EG-2: Address climate change

**Working definition:**
Reduce greenhouse gas concentrations and/or increase planetary resilience to climate disruption already in motion.

**Theory of change (refined):**

1. **Direct decarbonization impact at this scale:** Implausible. Joel's labor budget (10–29 hrs/week) and available capital ($1000 starter) cannot produce grid-scale renewables, industrial efficiency, or policy changes.

2. **Indirect leverage vectors:**
   - **Tooling for climate orgs:** Building software that helps climate-focused organizations (carbon accounting, impact measurement, operations) work better. Multiplier: 1 tool × 100 orgs × 10% efficiency gain = significant indirect impact.
   - **Institutional capital:** Funneling dollars to high-leverage giving orgs (Giving Green, Founders Pledge climate fund) where marginal dollars outperform most direct action.
   - **Public credibility/education:** Writing or speaking about climate science, communicating trade-offs with clarity. Multiplier: reaching 10,000 people with 1% opinion shift is worth the hour.

3. **Filtering for compound leverage:** Direct software contributions outperform isolated consulting. This favors building a tool that multiple organizations could use over one-off client work.

**Confidence:** 0.50 — indirect leverage exists but is diffuse; climate work is crowded with well-resourced competitors; the software-angle compounding claim is speculative.

**Open questions (linked to Q-003):**
- Is there a software/AI angle that compounds rather than just adds?
- At our resource scale, is dollars-to-impact higher than hours-to-impact in climate?

## EG-3: Support democratic institutions and constrain bad governance

**Working definition (reframed from "reduce bad governments and fascism"):**
Instead of targeting regime change, support institutions that constrain authoritarian power: journalism, human rights monitoring, dissident protection, civic literacy.
Reduce the number of people living under regimes that systematically violate human rights or suppress dissent.

**Theory of change (refined):**

This is the hardest end goal to act on without risking political influence (hard-refusal under ETHICS.md).
Reframing from "reduce bad governments" to "support constraining institutions" shifts focus to institutions rather than outcomes, reducing manipulation risk.

1. **Institutional support levers:**
   - Independent journalism: funding, technical support (secure publishing infrastructure)
   - Human rights monitoring: tools for data aggregation, evidence archiving, cross-border coordination
   - Dissident protection: secure communications tooling, anti-surveillance education
   - Civic literacy: education content for media literacy, understanding democratic institutions

2. **Why this avoids political influence:** These actions support *process* (transparency, evidence, individual autonomy to choose) rather than *outcomes* (which candidate wins, which regime falls). They respect autonomy while constraining coercive power.

3. **Resource constraint:** This space is well-funded by major foundations (Open Society, Google, Ford). Marginal impact of small new contribution is likely low unless we find a specific gap (tool, region, population) we can serve better.

**Confidence:** 0.40 — the reframing is cleaner but the crowded landscape makes differentiation hard; uncertain where small-scale contribution would be non-trivial.

**Open questions (linked to Q-004):**
- Is this end goal even appropriate to pursue actively, or should energy be redirected to other goals with clearer marginal impact?
- Is there a tool or population-focus where we have comparative advantage?

## EG-4: Reduce needless suffering, broadly

**Working definition:**
Suffering that arises from causes we know how to address — poverty, untreated illness, isolation, despair, factory-farm conditions for sentient beings.

**Theory of change (refined):**

Two vectors, with different time horizons:

1. **Give effectively now:** Marginal dollars routed to high-evidence charities (GiveWell Top Charities, GiveDirectly, Founders Pledge animal-welfare fund) outperform most direct action at our resource scale. Ratio: $1 can fund antimalarial nets, deworming, cash transfers with measurable impact. This is "immediate suffering reduction."

2. **Build capacity to give much more later:** Marginal hours given to building tools or platforms that help suffering-reducers do their work better—or that enable others to give more effectively—can compound. Examples: (a) cost-reduction tooling for nonprofits (grant management, impact measurement); (b) systems that scale expert knowledge (e.g., contemplative coaching under EG-1, which also reduces suffering); (c) public-impact communication that shifts norms and funding.

3. **Comparative advantage:** Joel has significant technical skills (software, AI); donating $1000 directly will fund ~$20k of malaria prevention (through GiveWell leverage). But building a tool that helps 50 nonprofits reduce operational overhead by 10% could enable them to redirect $500k over 5 years to direct work.

**Confidence:** 0.70 — the "give now" lever is proven; the "build capacity" lever is less certain but plausible and fits Joel's skills.

**Proposed resolution (to Q-005):**
Start with a 70/30 split: give $700 now to proven charities; allocate 30% of hours (3–9 hrs/week) to a capacity-building tool. Revisit quarterly.

**Open questions (linked to Q-005):**
- Right ratio of immediate giving vs. capacity-building at this scale?
- Which suffering-reducer tool would unlock the most value?
